Kripke, Analyticity, and Necessity

18 May 2026   1557 words   ~8 mins

It’s common lore that Kripke cleaves necessity from aprioricity via examples of a posteriori necessities. In doing so he rightfully distinguishes the epistemic distinction of a priori/a posteriori from the metaphysical distinction of necessity/possibility. Consider the case of a true identity claim made using different proper names:

  1. Hesperus = Hesperus
  2. By Necessitation, Necessarily Hesperus = Hesperus.1
  3. Hesperus = Phosphorus
  4. Therefore, Necessarily Hesperus = Phosphorus

“Hesperus = Phosphorus” expresses a necessary truth but one that we know a posteriori. So there are a posteriori necessities. So aprioricity is not interchangeable with necessity like was commonly presumed by the likes of the positivists.

Where does Analyticity fit in this picture? Kripke says:

At any rate, let’s just make it a matter of stipulation that an analytic statement is, in some sense, true by virtue of its meaning and true in all possible worlds by virtue of its meaning. Then something which is analytically true will be both necessary and a priori. (That’s sort of stipulative.)

So analyticity entails both aprioricity and necessity. Neither aprioricity nor necessity entails the other, so neither of them entail analyticity. Let’s call the view that analyticity implies necessity Kripke’s constraints on analyticity.2 It prima facie seems like a plausible constraint, but I don’t accept the view. For the purpose of showing why, I’ll suppose the view that a sentence is analytic just in case it’s synonymous with a logical truth.3

There’s a very good tool for separating necessity and analyticity; its name is indexicality. Kaplan provides an analysis of such indexicals with his Logic of Demonstratives (LD). The idea is we evaluate sentences with respect to a context parameter. Contexts can be taken as primitive, but typically they’re identified with tuples of the form \(\langle s, d, p, w\rangle\), where s is the speaker, d is a date, p a place, and w a possible world. Utterances with indexicals are evaluated relative to these contexts.

Now consider the sentence “I am here”; such a sentence is a logical truth in LD given that it is true in all models with regard to all contexts of utterance. If our criterion for analyticity is synonymity with a logical truth, then “I am here” is analytic.4 But in the context of its utterance the proposition it expresses is contingent.

There’s something that seems troubling with our example. Say you were to utter “I am here”, there’s a sense where that utterance is synonymous with a non-analytic utterance, namely “(name of reader) is in (location of reader)”, since the proposition expressed by both utterances is the same. It seems troublesome to suppose that a logical truth is synonymous with a synthetic statement. How should we deal with this?

Well, standard semantic theory has it that the meaning of a non-indexical declarative is the proposition it expresses. How do indexicals differ? In the case of sentences with indexicals (and other context-sensitive expressions) the meaning is a function of contexts of utterances to propositions. In a sense, indexicals have two kinds of meanings: what Kaplan dubs the ‘character’, that is, the rule that determines which proposition is expressed in a given context, and the content, the proposition expressed within a certain context of utterance.

We can give a sort of unificatory account of meaning: the meaning of a sentence S is a function from contexts C to propositions expressed by S in C. In cases where S is non-context sensitive the function yields the same proposition for every C. And so it’s clear that the sense of synonymy we’re interested in involves the unified account of meaning, concerned with the character of the sentence, not purely the content. Rightfully specifiying analyticity with regard to synonymity of character ought diffuse our worry.

So we have an analytic sentence that expresses a contingent proposition. Pretheoretic intuitions on the impossibility of such seems to be the natural result of failing to appreciate the distinction between sentence and proposition. Analyticity is a property of sentences; necessity a property of propositions.5 The fact that the bearers of analyticity and necessity differ doesn’t typically matter, but with indexicality the bearers come apart. The same sentence can express different propositions. This allows us to show that analyticity and necessity need not occur in all sentence-proposition pairs.

Just because indexicality is a good tool for showing us when analyticity and necessity come apart doesn’t mean indexicality is required. Consider the sentence “P if and only if actually P.”6 Such sentences are logical truths but there are instances where they express contingent truths.7 Consider the sentence “I’m 5'10 if and only if I’m actually 5'10” for example. It’s not necessary that I am 5'10, I could’ve easily been 5'6 (what a terrifying fate).

It seems like we should drop Kripke’s constraint on analyticity. There doesn’t seem to be much motivating such a constraint other than, prior to any reflection, it seems to us that all cases of analytic sentences express necessary propositions. But there was also a time prior to Kripke that the use of the a priori also indicated something was necessary. And anyways the situation seems worse worse for the analytic given the primary bearers differ unlike in the case of the a priori and the necessary.

  1. Given proper names rigidly designate this sentence is semantically de re, that is, the substitution of co-designating terms salva veritate is permitted. ↩︎

  2. For Kripke it’s less of a constraint on analyticity and more of a verbal stipulation. A few things can be said explicitly about his stipulation. Williamson points out that defining analyticity as the conjunction of aprioricity and necessity isn’t natural, as a disjunction of an a priori contingency with an unrelated a posteriori necessity would count as an analytic truth. There are ways to get around this but in the end it seems like a very unnatural way to posit analyticity. As an aside I kind of take issue with the phrase “in virtue of its meaning” with regard to defining analyticity. The purpose of the phrase seems to differentiate analytic sentences from synthetic statements that aren’t made true solely in virtue of their meaning but rather with the addition of the way the world is. I contend that there is no clear distinction to be made between analytic statements and synthetic statements on that basis. The idea here is that analytic truths and synthetic truths aren’t true in radically different ways. Both analytic and synthetic sentences are true in virtue of expressing a true proposition. Both are, in some sense, made true by how the world is. Consider the sentence “S means that P, and P”. It seems we can say this for any synthetic sentence “S” to explain its truth. But the same seems to hold for analytic sentences: “‘Vixens are female foxes’ means that vixens are female foxes, and vixens are female foxes.” You may ask what could the constraint analytic sentences pose on the world be? I’d say it poses a constraint on the world, just one we know easily to be met via the vehicle of logic. The cases the constraint excludes are merely those that are logically inconsistent. Any response to the effect that these constraints aren’t substantive and on that basis don’t count will be dialectically inert since it’s to suppose that logical truth isn’t substantive, which is what I am denying when I say they do impose constraints. ↩︎

  3. Dubbed “Frege-analyticity” by Boghossian. He distinguishes two possible readings of analyticity, a metaphysical and epistemological one, for the purposes of arguing that Quine’s critique of analyticity only undermines metaphysical analyticity. While not being a part of the metaphysical analytical category, those that are hostile to the notion of analyticity will probably reject Frege-analyticity, since it relies on synonymity, a notion Quine famously found to be a part of a circle of obscure notions none of which can be understood except in terms of the others (as an aside it also relies on logical truth, a notion Quine does admit). I suppose this matters very little if you agree with Strawson and Grice that Quine’s standards are too strict, in which case it’s a perfectly intelligible construction and seems reasonable as an account of analyticity. ↩︎

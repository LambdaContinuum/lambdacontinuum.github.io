A Quick Argument Contra Positivism

2025/06/11

Logical Positivism commits one to the thesis that meaningful thought is verifiable. An entailment of such a thesis is that all true propositions, in principle, are verifiable and therefore knowable. Here’s a short argument from Fitch’s Knowability Paradox that serves to prove positivism incorrect.

Assume, for reductio, that we know there are truths that no one knows and consider an epistemic logic that is at least system K4D:

  1. K(P ∧ ¬KP)

  2. Since our logic extends K, K(P ∧ ¬KP) ⊨ KP ∧ K¬KP

  3. By (2), KP ⊨ KKP

  4. This implies KKP ∧ K¬KP, which contradicts D.

  5. Therefore, we have derived a contradiction from our assumption, meaning it is impossible for the assumption to be true.

  6. Hence, ¬◊K(P ∧ ¬KP), that is, there are truths that are impossible to know.

Since we’ve demonstrated certain truths are impossible to know, one must either:

  1. Reject the assumption that there are truths we do not know,

  2. Reject the 4 and D, or

  3. Reject the notion that all true propositions are unknowable.

Option (1) is untenable as there are obviously truths we don’t know. Option (2) also doesn’t seem viable, if you know that P you know that you know that P (i.e. axiom 4) and if you know P you don’t know not P (i.e. axiom D) seem intuitive. The only option is to accept option (3) which contradicts positivism.

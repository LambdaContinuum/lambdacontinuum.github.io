Contra Contextualism Refuting Skepticism

2026/01/28

Radical skepticism can derive from multiple sources. One such source is the apparent paradox between one’s widespread everyday knowledge and the rational support provided by our perceptual experiences not seeming to favor the truth of our quotidian perceptual beliefs over radical skeptical scenarios such as being a brain in a vat, since the experiences a subject has under the radical skeptical scenarios are indistinguishable from everyday experiences ex hypothesi.

A well known response to this skeptical paradox is attributivist contextualism. The contextualist takes it that the word “knows” is context-sensitive and so the truth of various attributions of knowledge depends on the conversational context the word is uttered in. In the case of radical skepticism “know” is thought to pick out different epistemic standards in different contexts of attribution. Conversational contexts that have more demanding epistemic standards can render some knowledge attributions false even if they would be true in more lax contexts.

And so the skeptic is correct, but only because the assertions they’re making is made relative to a context with incredibly demanding epistemic standards. When we come back to normal contexts, everyday knowledge ascriptions are true. What contextualism is doing here is giving us sort of diagnostic story that shows us radical skepticism only seems like a paradox because we’ve not properly grasped the context-sensitive nature of the term “knows”.

A few things seem odd about this approach. Say it’s true that whenever we consider radical skepticism we are put into an epistemic context in which knowledge ascriptions are false. Note that this sort of answer is conceding to the skeptic, since their thesis is correct in the context of epistemic appraisal in which it is posed. This is intentional: the contextualist wants to claim that in the incredibly strict epistemic context the radical skeptic is correct, but once we return to everyday epistemic standards our knowledge assertions will go back to expressing truths. It’s problematic, though, given we’re conceding to the skeptic that their epistemic standards are stricter than ours and that the paradox disappears only because the radical skeptic is correct. Our only recourse is to return to quotidian epistemic standards and bar ourselves from entering the skeptic’s epistemic context. This sort of solution seems like it should only be a last resort. Here is something that strikes me as an obvious desideratum for a solution to radical skepticism, the skeptic is incorrect.

The contextualist’s solution also supposes the difficulty people have with dealing with the skeptic is the result of some mass semantic confusion. This is odd as philosophers don’t have similar difficulty understanding the context-sensitive nature of other supposedly analogous terms like “tall” and “fast.” No one is confused when you say a bike is fast in the context of men running but not fast in the context of cars racing. Contextualism attributes a widespread blindness to the meaning of terms to the subjects that use them. This isn’t a deal breaker but it should leave us a little suspicious.1

Most importantly it’s not clear how contextualism, as a semantic thesis, deals with what is fundamentally an issue of rational justification. Remember, the purported paradox arises when we grant that we have widespread everyday knowledge but that the rational support we have access to doesn’t privilege our ordinary knowledge over the radical skeptical scenarios. What claim does the contextualist deny? They don’t deny that we have widespread everyday knowledge, they’ll say we do in quotidian epistemic contexts. The contextualist, at best, will argue that the latter claim is problematic, because it explicitly makes reference to the skeptical scenario and brings us into a more demanding epistemic context. But to the contextualist the claim is inevitably true when one considers it. One needs to adds a further thesis about the rational support one has in quotidian contexts to address the skeptic since that’s what we’re concerned about, our rational grounding in the everyday context. The contextualist can’t say much about this on the basis of contextualism alone. This isn’t surprising though, like I said previously, contextualism is a semantic thesis and doesn’t say much about these fundamentally epistemic issues. But without saying anything about this, the radical skeptical paradox still needs to be addressed.

And so the crux of the issue is the skeptical paradox being raised doesn’t actually make reference to epistemic standards, what’s in play is a more general appeal to the inadequacy of the rational support available to our quotidian beliefs. It’s not a question about what “know” means, rather what’s being asked is a question about the nature of rational support. The contextualist can’t say much about this with contextualism alone. Say the contextualist does provide a further posit about the rational grounding one has in the ordinary context, for example, by dogmatically supposing there just are things we take to be true in the quotidian epistemic context that rationally ground everyday beliefs. Then what was the point of appealing to contextualism? It was totally superfluous. Once one starts to supplement contextualism to deal with the fundamentally epistemic issue at play, those supplemental posits are what do the anti-skeptic work, and contextualism becomes a superfluous element of the strategy.

So, it’s hard for me to see why radical skepticism should motivate us to adopt contextualism. This doesn’t mean there can’t be other considerations for adopting such a view, just that skepticism itself doesn’t do much to convince me.

  1. I think it’s a suspicious claim on its own but I was playing around with a more explicit argument about why it’s not the case. I decided to put it in the footnotes because I’m not entirely confident about it. Consider the thesis that one cannot fail to know the meanings of the signs one intends to utter, that is, meaning is transparent. I won’t argue for such a thesis here, but I’ve defended it as a natural result of taking certain Fregean datum seriously elsewhere. Now, granting that one cannot fail to know the meaning of the signs one intends to utter, and that analogous signs don’t have widespread confusion around such an integral part of their meaning, it’s unlikely that there would be widespread blindness to a crucial component of the meaning of “know,” namely its context sensitivity. A few things to note about this sort of inductive inference. It isn’t to argue that there aren’t some people who do in fact fail to know such a crucial component of the meaning of “know.” This is important to acknowledge because a prima facie response to such an argument might look like denying the transparency of meaning, since we do in fact observe actual agents who fail to know the meanings of signs they intend to utter. This misses the point of such a thesis (and the overall inductive inference being made), namely that failure to know the meaning of signs that one intends to utter is never on the basis of the constitution of meaning (which, as Bilgrami points out, one may observe in “secularized Platonic” accounts of the constitution of meaning within a linguistic community), and so failures are more akin to psychological failures. Think of it as the difference between the failure of an agent in an unideal epistemic environment versus the failure of an irrational agent. Thus, if meaning is transparent, we’ve reduced one such possibility of failure and have increased the likelihood that there isn’t widespread failure by removing a likely candidate for widespread failure. Now the major problem with this kind of inference, and why I’m hesitant about it, is it supposes a relatively strong thesis but only weakly probabilifies the conclusion. I don’t think the conclusion is made that much likelier in part due to my intuition that most failures of the kind are the result of psychology and not on the basis of the constitution of meaning. I’m not sure what to think either way. ↩︎

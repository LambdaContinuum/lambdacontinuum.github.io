Short Reflection on Reference and Donkeys

15 May 2026   780 words   ~4 mins
#anaphora #donkey sentences #indefinite descriptions #reference #compositionality #dynamic semantics #causal theory of reference #descriptivism #Geach #Quine

We use indefinites in day-to-day talk all the time; they’re the kind of phrases whose reference is ambiguous, general, or vague. Since philosophers of language love discussing reference, it’s a linguistic structure ripe for philosophical analysis.

Consider the sentence with an indefinite description ‘a(n) P is Q.’ Geach provides an analysis of the semantic content of such descriptions as mere existential quantification, \(\exists x(Px \land Qx)\).

Now, anaphora is a term with many meanings, but here we’ll use it to denote the linguistic phenomenon in which a phrase has its referent determined by an antecedent expression. An example of anaphora would be: ‘Ronald drove above the speed limit. He crashed.’ In the second sentence, ‘he’ refers to ‘Ronald’ from the previous sentence.

Let’s construct an instance of anaphora using an indefinite description, ‘a(n) P is Q. It is R.’ Following Quine’s approach to analyzing pronouns as bound variables yields the general existential formula \(\exists x(Px \land Qx \land Rx)\) as the truth conditions for anaphora with indefinite descriptions.

This analysis is problematic. Consider the donkey sentence: ‘If a farmer owns a donkey, he beats it.’ The antecedent of the conditional, ‘a farmer owns a donkey,’ includes an indefinite phrase, so our current analysis yields: \(\exists x \exists y(Fx \land Dy \land O(x, y) \to B(x, y))\). However, the truth conditions for a conditional are typically universally quantified: \(\forall x \forall y(Fx \land Dy \land O(x, y) \to B(x, y))\). If we use the former analysis, the sentence ‘If a farmer owns a donkey, he beats it’ could be consistent with ‘There’s a farmer who owns a donkey and doesn’t beat it.’ On the latter analysis, which invokes universal quantification, both sentences cannot be held consistently.

If the correct analysis of the donkey sentence interprets its pronouns as variables bound by universal quantifiers, then we cannot maintain the Geachian analysis without also rejecting the principle of compositionality, that is, the idea that the meaning of a sentence is determined by the meanings of its constituents along with its grammatical structure. This is clear, as the meaning of the antecedent indefinite phrase changes when it is embedded within the conditional. This presents a problem, since it is generally regarded as a desideratum of linguistic analysis that, assuming the phrase in question is extensional, the principle of compositionality should hold.

There have been various formal attempts to analyze this discourse. The general idea shared by these approaches is to treat anaphoric pronouns as bound variables and indefinites as existential quantifiers. However, to circumvent the problems this causes, they interpret existential quantifiers dynamically, introducing new objects available for reference and ensuring that universal quantification over assignment functions of individuals is involved in cases with connectives like conditionals. This approach accounts for donkey sentences and resolves the compositionality problem that arises.

These solutions are not without issues, as they employ what is known as existential closure, where an existential quantifier binds all free variables within its scope. This creates a problem in cases of pronominal contradiction. For example, if John asserts, ‘A man is running through the park,’ and Mary responds, ‘He is not running, but just walking,’ then, under this analysis, ‘he’ would be treated as an abbreviation for the indefinite description ‘a man is running through the park.’ This would mean Mary has contradicted herself by denying John’s assertion, which is clearly incorrect. Rather than abbreviating the indefinite description, ‘he’ evidently refers to the specific individual John had in mind when he used the indefinite phrase. However, due to the use of existential closure, the anaphoric pronoun ‘he’ in this context is treated as an abbreviation of the indefinite description.

This indicates that anaphoric pronouns are used in a referential manner, rather than as variables bound by dynamic existential quantifiers. What’s the upshot of all this? Consider someone choosing between a descriptive theory of meaning and a causal theory of meaning. According to a causal theory of reference, where a name refers to an object only if that object is the origin of a reference-preserving link from an initial ‘baptism’ of the expression to the speaker’s use of it, it is straightforward to explain why anaphoric pronouns are used in a referential manner. If the use of an anaphoric pronoun requires having a particular object in mind, it follows naturally that it will be used referentially. By contrast, if one endorses a descriptivist theory of meaning, where the meaning of an expression is given by the set of properties associated with it, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for its reference, it becomes difficult to account for the fact that anaphoric pronouns are used referentially rather than as variables bound by dynamic existential quantifiers.

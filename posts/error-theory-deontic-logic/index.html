Error Theory in Tension With Deontic Logic?

16 March 2026   738 words   ~4 mins
#error theory #deontic logic #metaethics #permissibility #SDL #moral realism #moral properties #normativity

I recently came across an interesting paper on formal objections to error theory, this was the gist of it. Error Theorists are typically construed as being committed to moral judgements being the sorts of judgements that have propositional content, but such judgements never come out true because the moral properties required to render them true are not instantiated.

Some believe error theory entails a formal inconsistency. Within SDL permission and impermission are interdefinable duals and so:

  1. \(\vDash \forall \phi (\phi \lor \neg \phi)\)
  2. \(P\psi \lor \neg P\psi\), from 1
  3. \(P\psi \lor I\psi\) since \(\neg P \psi = I \psi\)

From hereon out we’ll call (3) the Dual Schema (DS). The issue should be clear, the error theorist wants to say a moral judgement like “It is impermissible to steal candy from a baby” is false because the sort of property needed to render the judgement true, moral properties, do not obtain anywhere. But the falsity of that judgement alongside (DS) entails “It’s permissible to steal candy from a baby”, a judgement that seemingly instantiates the moral property of permissibility. So, error theory seems to contradict (DS), a logical truth of SDL.

The error theorist has a couple of moves here. A plausible one is just rejecting SDL, there are antecedent reasons to do so and I defend such a view on this blog but it’s not clear to me that that’s necessary. The author of the paper argues the objection is only superficially formal.

That might need a little more elaboration, what is the error theorist saying when they deny “It is impermissible to steal candy from a baby”? I’d say something like such a judgement is false because for it to be true there needs to be (stance-independent) reasons in favor of not stealing candy from a baby, but no such reasons of that kind exist. Given the (DS) the error theorist must then be committed to the truth of “It is permissible to steal candy from the baby” and the proponent of this objection thinks that’s an issue for the error theorist. But why? They’ll say “the truth of that judgement requires the moral property of permissibility and the error theorist rejects such properties” but note that the error theorist is objecting to something like the existence of reasons in favor of acting. It’s only an issue for the error theorist to endorse “It is permissible to steal candy from a baby” if the judgement means something like there’s a reason in favor of the act being permissible but the (DS) doesn’t suppose any such analysis. Suppose instead that to say “It is permissible to steal candy from a baby” is just to say that there are no reasons in favor of not stealing candy from a baby.

So we have two analyses of what it means to say “It’s permissible to steal candy from the baby”, call the one that supposes the existence of reasons in favor of acting the (P+) variant and the one that supposes there are no reasons in favor of acting the (P-) variant. The error theorist is clearly committed to a contradiction in affirming (P+) given they think all such judgements are false in virtue of supposing the existence of reasons that don’t obtain, but there’s no issue for the error theorist in affirming (P-) since the claim doesn’t suppose such reasons.1 And so there’s only a contradiction for the error theorist if (DS) entails (P+) instead of (P-) but why think that? The objection being raised isn’t purely formal, it implicitly supposes the dual schema involves a certain analysis of permissibility that supposes the sort of properties the error theorist wishes to reject, but the error theorist can just reject that.

So say we have two (DS) variants, (DS+) supposes permission is analyzed in line with (P+) while (DS-) supposes permission is analyzed in line with (PS-). SDL says nothing about what variant (DS) is supposed to endorse, but for SDL to show a contradiction in error theory (DS) has to be (DS+). Either way, there isn’t a clear formal contradiction for the error theorist in adopting SDL, they just need to deny their opponents framing of judgements about permission involving the existence reasons.

  1. In fact, the judgement supposes that such reasons don’t exist. A corollary of this is all judgements about the permission of acts come out true but I don’t see why that would be an issue. ↩︎

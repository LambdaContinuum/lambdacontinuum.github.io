Is existence a predicate?

2025/09/14

You hear this argument frequently when discussing the ontological argument for God. The theist may claim that existence is a perfection, while the skeptic counters by arguing that perfections must be properties and to be a property one must be able to predicate it of an object. However, ‘existence is not a predicate,’ merely a prerequisite for properties.

Okay, but what exactly does it mean for existence to be (or not to be) a predicate? To me it seems unclear between various interpretations.

It is trivial to construct a first-order monadic predicate, termed ‘exists,’ such that exists := λx (∃y y = x). This ‘exists’ predicate is true of and only of things that exist and will abide by the syntactic rules of a predicate in first-order logic. So, why would it be incorrect to say that existence is a predicate?

Well, the dictum that existence isn’t a predicate originates with Kant, and he was writing before the progress in a logic of quantification that Frege, Russell, Quine, and others contributed to. It’s reasonable to assume that when Kant claimed existence isn’t a predicate, he meant something different.

There are a few possible things Kant may have had in mind when he said that existence isn’t a predicate.

At the time, traditional formal logic was relegated to Port Royal and Aristotelian syllogism. Such formal logic viewed existence as explicated in terms of copulas like ‘is’ or ‘are.’ Consider traditional examples of syllogistic logic, such as ‘Socrates is a man’ or ‘Men are mortal.’ If these sentences are true, they imply that both men and Socrates exist. So, existence is not a real predicate but is merely derivatively implied by the copula. Existence is merely a matter of the logic of the copula. He may also have meant that, whereas conceiving of a red apple adds something to the concept of an apple, conceiving of an existing apple doesn’t add anything because the mental images of an existing apple and a non-existing apple are the same. Thus, existence isn’t a predicate.

But in both cases, when Kant says that existence isn’t a predicate, he seems to be addressing something very different from what someone who constructs the predicate above is claiming. When Kant says existence isn’t a predicate, he is suggesting that within the framework of Aristotelian syllogistic logic, existence isn’t a predicate or that existence does not “add” to the concept of a thing. Meanwhile, those who endorse the trivial construction are simply asserting that a predicate named ’existence’ can be constructed in a predicate logic, which is true only of existing things. They are addressing two different questions.

Let’s take someone from a post-Frege world of logic. Gilbert Ryle denies that existence is a predicate. What does he mean? The idea is that existence differs significantly from other ordinary terms used in the predicate position. Consider the following sentences:

  1. ‘Brown cows exist.’

  2. ‘Purple cows don’t exist.’

  3. ‘Brown cows flourish.’

  4. ‘Purple cows don’t flourish.’

The idea is that we are systematically misled into treating sentences like (1) and (2) analogously to (3) and (4) due to superficial grammatical resemblance, since ’exists’ appears in the predicate position. However, there is a major difference between existence claims and other predicate claims, such as ‘flourish.’ The former are translatable into quantificational sentences like ‘Some cows are brown’ and ‘It is false that some cows are purple.’ In translation, the word ’exists’ disappears from the predicate position, with its function being accomplished by the quantifier. In contrast, ordinary predicates like ‘flourish’ and ‘red’ are genuine predicates, as they cannot be translated into sentences where they no longer take a predicate position. The idea is that every existence claim is merely a covert quantificational claim, and hence ’exists’ is a bogus predicate. The proper role of existence is played by quantification and not predication.

If this is what’s at dispute, then it’s not even clear that Ryle disagrees with someone who claims existence is a predicate because they can construct a ’exists’ predicate. What’s actually in contention is whether or not it is a ‘genuine’ predicate. One could endorse that ’exists’ is a predicate but not a ’true’ predicate in the sense that Ryle describes.

It seems to me that asking whether existence is a predicate is essentially pointless without further clarification of what is meant when one asks.
